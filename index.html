Biography

About Me

I've always learned fastest when I'm slightly in over my head, so I keep putting myself there on purpose. This instinct to find the hardest problem in the room and talk my way to a seat has shaped everything, and brought me across fields and countries. It took me from graph neural networks and privacy-preserving machine learning at MIT CSAIL, to training humanoid robots to walk with reinforcement learning at AgiBot in Shanghai, to building cancer prediction models alongside clinicians at Cedars-Sinai. Now I'm a first-year Electrical Engineering student at Georgia Tech, working on egocentric human-to-robot transfer at the Robot and Reasoning Lab.

Getting constantly hit by new things has taught me to get really good at ramping up fast. Every new room I've entered has dropped me into a new codebase, a new domain, and a new team that was already moving. Each time I had to earn my place by learning and innovating, not by catching up quietly. That pattern has made me strong in machine learning and reinforcement learning, where I've gone from reading papers to running experiments to submitting work to top venues like Nature Machine Learning and Robotics: Science and Systems. My go-to tools are Python and C++, and I've built with them from low-level motor control on embedded hardware to large-scale distributed training jobs on GPU clusters.

Throughout all of my work, I've focused on one thing: I want to build intelligent systems that don't just add a few accuracy points on a benchmark, but work in operating rooms, on factory floors, in the places where getting it wrong actually matters. Crucial research is so often never implemented in an impactful way, and I aim to help change that.

When I'm not debugging training runs, I'm racing drones, playing competitive Splatoon, or assembling a mechanical keyboard with more parts than some of my research projects.

EgoVerse

EgoVerse is an open ecosystem for curating, accessing, and learning from human egocentric video data to accelerate robot learning. The core challenge in robotics today is that training dexterous manipulation policies requires enormous amounts of robot-specific demonstration data — expensive, slow, and hard to scale. EgoVerse attacks this from a different angle: instead of collecting more robot data, we leverage the vast wealth of human egocentric video that already exists.

As a core member of the EgoVerse Team at Georgia Tech's Robot Learning and Reasoning Lab, I spearheaded the development of human-to-robot transfer learning frameworks, partnered with Scale AI to build custom data conversion and training pipelines processing 250+ hours of video, and architected high-performance Zarr datasets enabling seamless handling of up to 1,000 hours of multimodal data. The trained cross-domain models can perform complex dexterous tasks by learning directly from human demonstrations, significantly reducing reliance on robot-specific data.

PyTorch GNN Privacy-Preserving ML Nature Machine Intelligence

ConstellationNet

A novel graph neural network clustering framework developed at MIT CSAIL, proposing state-of-the-art methods for training stability and cluster quality in privacy-preserving settings. Built with custom loss functions, aggregations, and message passing through residual edge connections, with group-based clipping and dynamic model reinitialization. Published in Nature Machine Intelligence.
